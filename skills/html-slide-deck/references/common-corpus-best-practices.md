# Common Corpus Best Practices

Use this reference when selecting or evolving templates from historical slide material. It stores sanitized patterns only: no client names, no copied slide text, no private screenshots, and no local source paths that would be required by other users.

## Source Model

Historical material should be treated as two different sources:

- Complete decks: use their `outline` structure to learn story logic and section rhythm.
- Single-page assets: use their `layout`, `background`, `contentTags`, and `themeTags` to learn reusable page forms.

Do not copy business copy, customer identity, screenshots, project-specific diagrams, or one-off title phrasing into a distributed skill. Convert them into abstract rules, page types, and placeholder examples.

## Presales Support Content Logic

Presales decks usually need decision support and solution confidence. A strong default story is:

1. Relationship or opening context: why this conversation matters now.
2. Customer current state: goals, constraints, pain points, or existing foundation.
3. Top-level design: overall blueprint, target architecture, or planning principle.
4. Solution modules: break the proposal into 3-6 named capability groups.
5. Evidence and feasibility: cases, landing examples, technical architecture, security, ecosystem, or rollout proof.
6. Implementation path: phasing, governance, training, support, and delivery rhythm.
7. Summary and co-building statement: close on shared direction, not a feature list.

Presales pages should avoid becoming a product brochure. Each solution module should connect back to the customer's stated goal, operating scenario, or decision concern.

Common presales page types:

- Customer-context opening page.
- Current-state and challenge page.
- Top-level blueprint page.
- Two-category or three-category capability card page.
- Architecture or deployment page.
- Case/evidence page.
- Implementation roadmap or timeline page.
- Governance/training/support page.
- Summary page.

## Industry Conference Content Logic

Industry conference decks usually need a public argument, not a sales sequence. A strong default story is:

1. Macro question: begin with an industry-level contradiction, trend, or inflection point.
2. Paradigm shift: explain the new technology, policy, data, or productivity logic.
3. Healthcare implication: translate the shift into medical roles, workflows, or system change.
4. Framework: give the audience a memorable model, such as stages, layers, loops, or role evolution.
5. Practice: show product, scenario, architecture, or case evidence as proof of the framework.
6. Future claim: end with a concise position the audience can remember.

Conference pages can be more conceptual and visually rhythmic than presales pages. Use quote pages, big-number/data hero pages, timeline/history pages, and full-bleed visual pages when they clarify the argument.

Common industry conference page types:

- Opening thesis page.
- Macro trend or contradiction page.
- Technology evolution timeline.
- Framework/model page.
- Scenario reinterpretation page.
- Data hero or quote page.
- Product/practice evidence page.
- Future claim or memorable closing page.

## Visual Directions

Use two visual directions as first-class variants for presales support and industry conference scenes.

Dark technology:

- Best for AI, architecture, infrastructure, cloud, data platform, security, and high-impact conference talks.
- Use deep navy/black-blue backgrounds, cyan/blue highlights, glow lines, glass panels, network/grid motifs, and stronger contrast.
- Keep content density under control; dark pages become heavy quickly when every card glows.

Light business:

- Best for customer planning, formal communication, strategy recommendations, training, and long decks that require readability.
- Use white or pale blue backgrounds, clear blue accents, restrained gradients, soft grids, and business-like cards/tables.
- Maintain visual energy with section dividers, large model diagrams, and periodic data/quote pages.

When a user asks not to repeat a previous look, stay in the same scene but change the variant, page rhythm, cover treatment, diagram language, and accent system.

## Layout Library Guidance

Use the single-page asset taxonomy as reusable layout choices, not as rigid templates.

- Two-category: compare two forces, roles, tracks, strategies, options, or responsibility areas. The internal count may be 1+1, 1+2, 2+2, image+text, table+text, or mixed.
- Three-category: use for strategy pillars, capability groups, phased logic, or value dimensions.
- Four-category: use for operating models, matrix-like capability sets, role groups, or complete coverage maps.
- Timeline: use for development history, rollout path, evolution stages, or implementation plan.
- Table: use when comparison, requirements, responsibility, or plan detail is the main content.
- Chart: use when data trend or quantitative comparison is the core evidence.
- Map: use for regional coverage, service network, customer distribution, or rollout geography.
- Section divider: use to reset rhythm and announce a new argument stage.

Treat each layout as a container. The generated deck may alter card count, media placement, and module size when the user's content requires it.

## Updating This Corpus

When reviewing more historical material:

1. Extract the complete deck outline first.
2. Classify it by scene, visual direction, audience, and dominant story arc.
3. Record only reusable patterns: section sequence, page type rhythm, layout category, and design principle.
4. Generalize any customer/project-specific title into an abstract rule.
5. If a pattern appears repeatedly, promote it into a template variant's `content.md` or `design.md`.
6. If a pattern is useful but cross-template, keep it in this common reference.

Do not let the corpus freeze creativity. Use it to inherit proven logic, then adapt or extend the template when the new deck has a different audience, purpose, or material shape.
